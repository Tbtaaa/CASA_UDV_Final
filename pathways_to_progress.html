Motivation

Every morning, close to 600,000 children travel to secondary schools across London.

But, how does where they live shape the schools, and opportunities, they can access?

Most students can reasonably access a secondary school near home.
However, not all schools offer the same opportunities.
In reality, access to choice schools (and better opportunities) often depends on connectivity — where you live shapes what you can reach.

02 — Connectivity

Some children have dozens of schools within reach

A child living in inner London — like Clapham North in Lambeth — can reach up to 96 schools within a 45-minute transit journey. Multiple tube lines and bus corridors fan outward in every direction.

02 — Connectivity

Others, including some in outer London, face far fewer options

In a bus-only outer London neighbourhood — like Harold Hill in Havering — that same 45-minute window reaches just 7 schools. The transit network simply does not extend here with the same density.

01 — Spatial clustering

Pockets of compounded disadvantage exist across London

In outer London — east, north-east, and north-west — children face both high transit times and high deprivation , a double penalty where geography compounds disadvantage.

In contrast, west and south-west London shows short commutes with low deprivation reinforcing one another — families live on the doorstep of the city's best schools, underwritten by suburban affluence and car ownership.

Most strikingly in inner east London, we unexpectedly find fast access and high deprivation. If students here are still falling behind, the barrier likely isn't transport — it's likely social, administrative, or admission-based.

Red clusters mark where disadvantage is compounded. Hover over clusters to view details.

03 — Income Context

Lower incomes limit the ability to overcome access differences in these areas

On average, households in areas with High Transit Times and High Deprivation earn 25% less than the London average.

Median annual household income after housing costs, aggregated by LISA cluster.

This material difference affects families' ability to manage longer commutes. Lower-income households are less able to rely on private transport or widen their school choices, making them more dependent on public transit.

These children face a compounded challenge: longer travel times combined with fewer resources to mitigate them.

04 — Borough patterns

Borough-level patterns highlight where action is most needed

The concentration of high-disadvantage neighbourhoods is not evenly spread. Boroughs such as Newham, Enfield, and Islington contain the highest shares of affected LSOAs.

Outer boroughs
The issue is primarily structural — weaker transport links increase travel times across entire areas.
Inner boroughs (e.g. Islington)
Pockets of deprivation persist despite strong overall accessibility — highlighting inequalities within well-connected areas.

→ Policy responses need to be place-specific: transport improvements in outer London, targeted support and school access strategies in inner-city areas.

Reflections

Mind the Gap: Reflections on School Access and Opportunity in London

01
Access vs. Choice
How we define access and choice shapes how we understand school accessibility. After accounting for choice, subtle inequalities remain — often reflecting a trade-off between where students live and the opportunities they can reach.
02
Bridging the Gap
Ensuring fair access for the next generation requires a concerted effort. Transport authorities should promote affordable and sustainable travel, especially in outer areas. Local authorities can step up targeted assistance to lower-income families, whilst ensuring good school choice in more deprived areas.
03
Securing Future Equity
As pupil demand gradually falls and school closures become an eventuality, how local authorities think about reallocating resources must keep equitable travel to schools as a planning priority for residents.
